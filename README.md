> **Peirce, Charles Sanders (1867).** "On a New List of Categories." *Proceedings of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences* **7**, 287–298. Peirce's first systematic presentation of the triadic sign structure and his three universal phenomenological categories: **Firstness** (quality in itself, pure potentiality, the mode of being of a quality before anything in the universe is yet so qualified), **Secondness** (reaction, existence, the brute fact of one thing being what it is in relation to another), and **Thirdness** (representation, mediation, the mode of bringing a first and a second into relation through a third). All genuine triadic relations are irreducible to dyadic or monadic ones — Peirce's Reduction Thesis, later formally proved by Robert Burch (1991) and Correia-Pöschel (2006). Every sign is a genuine triadic relation between representamen, object, and interpretant.

> **Peirce, Charles Sanders (1878a).** "How to Make Our Ideas Clear." *Popular Science Monthly* **12**, 286–302. The canonical statement of the pragmatic maxim: "Consider what effects, that might conceivably have practical bearings, we conceive the object of our conception to have. Then, our conception of those effects is the whole of our conception of the object." The pragmatic maxim defines meaning operationally: the meaning of a concept IS the totality of its conceivable practical consequences. No practical difference, no conceptual difference. This maxim dissolves pseudo-problems (disputes about concepts with no differential practical consequences) and grounds all genuine inquiry in observable, testable effects.

> **Peirce, Charles Sanders (1878b).** "The Probability of Induction." *Popular Science Monthly* **12**, 705–718; "Deduction, Induction, and Hypothesis." *Popular Science Monthly* **13**, 470–482. Peirce distinguishes three irreducible modes of inference: **deduction** (necessary, demonstrative: applies a rule to a case to derive a result), **induction** (experimental, self-correcting: generalizes from samples to rules), and **abduction** (hypothesis-forming: infers the best explanatory hypothesis from a surprising fact). Abduction is the only form of inference that genuinely extends knowledge: "The only way to make progress in science is by abduction." Abduction is the first stage of inquiry; induction tests and stabilizes abductive hypotheses.

> **Peirce, Charles Sanders (1903).** "Pragmatism as a Principle and Method of Right Thinking" (Harvard Lectures on Pragmatism). Repr. in *The Essential Peirce*, vol. 2. Peirce distinguishes **pragmatism** (his own doctrine, renamed pragmaticism to distinguish it from James's subjectivized version) from other philosophies. The community of inquiry is the proper subject of semiotic logic: truth is "what the community of inquiry is fated to arrive at in the long run." Peirce's **continuum theory**: the process of semiosis (sign interpretation) is potentially infinite — every interpretant becomes a new sign generating further interpretants. The sign relation is irreducibly triadic and processual, not dyadic and static.

> **Peirce, Charles Sanders (c.1902–1910).** *The New Elements of Mathematics* (ed. Carolyn Eisele, 4 vols., 1976). Eisele's edition of Peirce's mathematical manuscripts establishes his formal contributions to relational logic, the algebra of logic (developing Boole's and De Morgan's work), the theory of graphs (existential graphs — his graphical notation for logic that anticipated later developments in proof theory and category theory), and the foundations of continua. His existential graphs provide a diagrammatic formal calculus in which the rules of inference are spatial transformations — the first visual logic in the Western tradition.

> **Russell, Bertrand, and Whitehead, Alfred North (1910–1913).** *Principia Mathematica*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press (3 vols.). The definitive attempt to demonstrate that all of classical mathematics is derivable from purely logical axioms. Key formal contributions: (1) **Ramified type theory**: a hierarchical stratification of propositional functions into types (preventing self-referential definitions that generate paradoxes) and orders (preventing impredicative definitions); (2) **Theory of descriptions**: logical analysis of definite descriptions as incomplete symbols, eliminable through contextual definition; (3) **Propositional functions**: open formulae $\varphi(x)$ that become propositions when a value is substituted for the variable. *PM* laid the formal foundations for Gödel's incompleteness theorems (1931), Church's lambda calculus, and the Curry-Howard correspondence (propositions-as-types principle) that underlies modern functional programming languages and dependent type theory.

> **Russell, Bertrand (1905).** "On Denoting." *Mind* **14**(56), 479–493. Russell's theory of descriptions shows that surface grammatical form conceals logical form: "The present king of France is bald" appears to denote an individual but logically decomposes as "There exists exactly one $x$ such that $x$ is currently king of France, and $x$ is bald" — which is false (not meaningless) since France has no king. The logical form is fully determined by its truth conditions, independent of whether the denoted object exists. The distinction between grammatical and logical form becomes foundational for analytic philosophy and the formal semantics of natural language.

> **Saint-Simon, Henri de (1813 / 1975).** *Mémoire sur la Science de l'Homme* and *Introduction aux travaux scientifiques du XIXe siècle*. In *Oeuvres de Claude-Henri de Saint-Simon*. Paris: Anthropos. Saint-Simon proposed that "social physics" — a positive science of society modeled on Newtonian mechanics — could derive the laws governing social organization from observation, exactly as Newton derived the laws of celestial mechanics from observation. Society, like a physical system, has invariable laws that could be discovered by systematic inquiry and applied to social planning. This program, transmitted directly to his secretary Auguste Comte, becomes the foundation of sociology and of positivism.

> **Comte, Auguste (1830–1842).** *Cours de philosophie positive* (6 vols.). Paris: Bachelier. Trans. Harriet Martineau as *The Positive Philosophy of Auguste Comte* (1853). The foundational positivist text. Two core claims: (1) **Law of three stages**: every domain of knowledge passes through theological (explanations by divine will), metaphysical (explanations by abstract essences), and positive (explanations by invariable laws derived from observation and experiment) stages of development; (2) **Hierarchy of sciences**: the sciences are ordered by increasing complexity and decreasing generality — mathematics, astronomy, physics, chemistry, biology, sociology — with each science depending on and presupposing all preceding it. Sociology (initially called "social physics") is the most complex and the last to achieve positive status.

> **Eisele, Carolyn (1979).** *Studies in the Scientific and Mathematical Philosophy of Charles S. Peirce*. The Hague: Mouton. Eisele's comprehensive study establishes Peirce as a practicing scientist (30 years with the U.S. Coast Survey), a rigorous mathematician, and a logician whose contributions to the algebra of relations, existential graphs, and the foundations of continua are formally significant independent of their semiotic context. Eisele's edition of Peirce's mathematical manuscripts (*New Elements of Mathematics*, 4 vols.) makes accessible a body of formal work that anticipated much of 20th-century mathematical logic.

## Module A — Mathematical Background

**A1. The triadic sign as the minimal coordination relation.** Peirce's reduction thesis establishes that genuinely triadic relations are irreducible to dyadic or monadic ones. A sign relation requires exactly three terms: representamen $R$ (the form the sign takes), object $O$ (what the sign refers to), and interpretant $I$ (the effect produced in a mind or system by the sign's relation to its object). The sign brings the interpretant into the same sort of correspondence with the object as the sign itself stands to the object. No two-term relation achieves this: a mere causal link ($R \to O$) has no interpretant; a mere mental state ($I$ alone) has no representamen. Three is the minimum. In ERI: the minimal coordination event requires exactly three terms — contributor $a_t$ (representamen), artifact state $X_{t-1}$ (object), and effect on subsequent contributors $a_s$ (interpretant). The conditioning clause $\mid X_{t-1}$ IS the triadic sign relation: it brings $a_s$ into correspondence with $X_{t-1}$ through $a_t$'s mediation.

**A2. Peirce's three categories as the CHORD phase structure.** Peirce's three categories pervade all phenomena:

- **Firstness**: pure quality, potentiality, the mode of being in itself with no reference to anything else. The uninterpreted gradient $g_t$ before its Fisher-conditioned context is established — pure potential, not yet a coordination event.
- **Secondness**: brute reaction, dyadic existence, one thing impacting another without mediation. The raw outer product $g_t g_t^\top$ — the Fisher trace before accumulation — a dyadic mark of the gradient's local impact.
- **Thirdness**: mediation, representation, the mode of bringing first and second into relation. The natural gradient $F^{-1} g_t$ — the Fisher-conditioned update — which mediates between the current gradient (firstness) and the accumulated Fisher history (secondness) to produce a coordination event of genuine thirdness.

The CHORD 16-stage pipeline IS the instantiation of Peirce's three categories in fixed-point arithmetic: Stages 1–4 (outer-product accumulation) operate at Secondness; Stages 5–8 (Jacobi SVD) construct the Thirdness structure from the Secondness input; Stage 15 (ker$(F)$ zeroing) eliminates the Firstness-only directions that have not risen to Secondness or Thirdness.

**A3. Abduction, induction, deduction as the three ERI reasoning modes.** Peirce's three forms of inference map exactly onto three phases of the ERI pipeline:

| Peircean inference | ERI operation | CHORD stage |
|---|---|---|
| **Abduction** (hypothesis generation from surprising fact) | $\Delta\text{rank}(F) = +1$: new Fisher eigenvector from unexpected data | PRIMA: cross-register injection of new col$(F)$ direction |

Inquiry always begins with abduction (a surprising data point generates a new hypothesis = a new Fisher eigenvector). Induction tests the hypothesis (accumulates further gradient outer products to see if the new eigenvector stabilizes). Deduction derives the consequences (the natural gradient applies the stabilized Fisher geometry to the current gradient).

**A4. The pragmatic maxim as the operational definition of $G_{\text{coord}}$.** Peirce's pragmatic maxim states: the meaning of a concept is the totality of its conceivable practical consequences. Two concepts that produce identical practical consequences are identical concepts. In ERI: the pragmatic meaning of a contribution $a_t$ IS $I(a_t; a_s \mid X_{t-1})$ — the contribution's effect on the conditional mutual information between subsequent contributions $a_s$, given the accumulated artifact state $X_{t-1}$. ### Identity 1 — The Peircean Triadic Sign IS the ERI Conditioning Clause; Semiosis IS $G_{\text{coord}}$; The Community of Inquiry IS the ERI Knowledge Commons; The Pragmatic Maxim IS the Operational Definition of Coordination; Peirce's Continuum IS the Open Pan-Genome of FERN

**The triadic sign IS the conditioning clause.** Peirce defines the sign as: "A sign is something, A, which brings something, B, its interpretant sign determined or created by it, into the same sort of correspondence with something, C, its object, as that in which itself stands to C." Three terms, one irreducible relation. In ERI: $A$ = the contribution $a_t$ (representamen); $C$ = the artifact state $X_{t-1}$ (object); $B$ = the effect on subsequent contributors $a_s$ (interpretant). Restore the object: the triadic sign relation operates, $G_{\text{coord}} > 0$, semiosis proceeds.

**Semiosis IS $G_{\text{coord}}$ over time.** Peirce's semiosis is the open-ended process by which interpretants become new signs generating further interpretants — potentially without termination. The chain of signs $A_1 \to A_2 \to A_3 \to \ldots$ is unbounded: "The series of interpretants terminates only in a sign of the kind which is its own interpretant." In ERI: $G_{\text{coord}} = \sum_t I(a_t; a_s \mid X_{t-1})$ IS the running sum of the semiotic chain — each new contribution $a_t$ IS a new interpretant-sign, producing effects on subsequent contributions $a_s$ through the accumulated object-state $X_{t-1}$. **The community of inquiry IS the ERI commons.** Peirce held that truth is "the opinion which is fated to be ultimately agreed to by all who investigate." This is not subjective consensus but the ideal limit of self-correcting inquiry by an indefinitely extended community of investigators. No individual reaches truth; the community of inquiry does. In ERI: $\lim_{t \to \infty} G_{\text{coord}}(X_t) = G_{\text{coord}}^*$ — the asymptotic coordination value approached by the knowledge commons as $|X_t| \to \infty$.
